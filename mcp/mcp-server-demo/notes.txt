Bears are found on every continent except Antarctica and Australia, with 8 species worldwide including polar bears, brown bears, and black bears.
Despite their large size, bears can run up to 35 mph (56 km/h) - faster than most humans can sprint.
Bears have an excellent sense of smell - up to 7 times better than a bloodhound - and can detect food from miles away.
Most bears are omnivores, eating both plants and meat, though polar bears are primarily carnivorous and giant pandas eat almost exclusively bamboo.
Bears don't truly hibernate - they enter a state called torpor where their heart rate and breathing slow down but they can still wake up if disturbed.
Baby bears are called cubs and are typically born blind, hairless, and weighing less than a pound, despite their mothers weighing hundreds of pounds.
Bears are excellent swimmers and climbers - even adult black bears can climb trees with ease using their sharp claws.
The largest bear species is the polar bear, with males weighing up to 1,700 pounds, while the smallest is the sun bear at around 140 pounds.
Bears have been important in human culture for thousands of years, appearing in folklore, mythology, and as symbols of strength and wilderness.
Many bear species are threatened by habitat loss and climate change, with polar bears being particularly vulnerable due to melting Arctic ice.
